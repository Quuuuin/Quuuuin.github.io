Touch Me Not

The Touch Me Not project aims to provide an opportunity for most people who don't have synaesthesia, an ability that only a very few people have. Synaesthesia is a neurological phenomenon in which stimulation of one sensory or cognitive pathway leads to automatic, involuntary experiences in a second sensory or cognitive pathway.
The project focuses on the auditory-tactile synaesthesia experience. We designed a partitioned panel with different materials covering the surface. When the user touches the materials, different melodies will be played depending on the materials. Users can create their chords by touching multiple areas simultaneously, giving them the experience of making music through their tactile feedback.
My role:UX Researcher, UX Designer, Music designer, Craftsman
Tool used: Ableton Live, Excel, Messanger
What I did: User Experience research, design, test & evaluation, Music design, Constructing the device
Duration: 1 Augest - 19 October 2022 as part of Course Design Computing Studio 3 - Build at UQ View the Project Documentation View the Kickstarter video

Background

In this studio, we formed an interdisciplinary team to design, build, test, evaluate our projects based on a supplied set of requirements provided by teaching team. Through this studio, we developed skills in project management, effective communication, critical thinking, and self-reflection.
Therefore, the background research and proposal are readily available, and we conducted minimal research before entering the development stage. To better understand the synaesthetic experience, our team conducted online interviews with four volunteers who have synaesthesia.

Justification-Function

From the interview, we found that the synaesthesia experiences are very personal and might not be understandable for general people. So it might not be a good idea to try to directly copy the synesthesia experiences from this small group of people to the public.
We looked at other synesthesia-related devices, most connecting images and music. The idea is that painters bind to instruments and automatically paint when the tools are played.
Our team decided to create an auditory-tactile synesthesia device based on two factors. Firstly, we were inspired by the idea of creating a more immersive music experience for people. Secondly, we wanted to explore the potential of technology to simulate synesthesia and how it could enhance the music-listening experience.
In summary, we have decided to design this device as an auditory-tactile synesthesia-based soundboard/music synthesizer .
We decided there would be 6 textures for users to feel in the final prototype, each connected to a music clip. There is background music looping if the device is turned on. The music clips linking to the materials can be seen as voice parts, and users can create their music by adding, removing, and changing those voice parts.
Users can control the device by touching the materials. They can control which voice part will be played by touching the corresponding materials. Users can control the volume and the filter parameters added to the music clip by touch with different strengths. This control allows multi-level play. With normal interaction strength, the device could create a good sound, while skilled users could make better music with proper timing and power of touch.

Justification-Industrial Design

Our initial design envisioned the device as a midi controller-like model with two main components: a square box for the materials and a transparent container for the sensors at the bottom. This design intuitively showcases the correlation between tactile sensations and music. The transparent acrylic container also provides a clear view of the device's internal structure.
During the subsequent development, we conducted a small-scale evaluation. We found that the first version of the design might amplify users' perception of vision, causing them to overlook the tactile feedback associated with the materials and link the music to specific substances. To avoid this issue, we updated the design and designed it as a blind box. This form avoids visual interference with tactile feedback and gives users a sense of connection and surprise during the interaction.

Justification-Music

At the beginning of the design, we created sound effects connected to each material and edited them into a rhythmic combination without involving any background music production. However, our internal evaluation found that piecing together these effects as rhythmic percussion made the entire music sound very noisy, chaotic, and somewhat dull.
Therefore, we chose a compromise solution that may reduce the possibilities for creation. We set a default background sound for the device. Users can touch different materials to add percussion patterns or accompaniment melodies to the background. Although we created music according to our imagined tactile sense, which is very subjective, we need to verify and iterate it in the evaluation stage.
In the second round of development, we decided to provide users with more control because we wanted to offer a higher level of experience and make the device more fun and creative. Because using different levels of force to touch materials brings slight sensory differences, we decided that the music should also sound different.Therefore, we gave each music clip more possibilities.
Based on the feeling of the materials when touched with different forces, we added sound effects that change according to the intensity of the touch in the clip. The parameters of these sound effects vary with the strength of the touch. For example, when the user presses hard, the part of the music associated with wood becomes more piercing.

User test & Evaluation

We first asked our testers to listen to each piece of music and describe the emotional experience that the music gave them, like sadness or happiness.
We designed it like this because when we first conducted a small-scope pre-test, we found it challenging for people to associate a piece of music with the sense of touch. The testers tended to mistake the auditory-textile connection for the sound the material could make. So we improved our test process, trying to guide them toward more abstract concepts. Subsequent tests verified that our process improvements were practical, and the testers were more active in expressing their thoughts. They could make connections to a broader range of material tactile sensations.
After the first test task, we give a list of the material that our creators corresponded to when they composed the music and ask the testers to connect the material that they feel has a connection to music.We designed tests like this because of the limited course time, and we could not conduct more rounds of testing and music iterations. We tried to narrow down the vast and varied range of answers by using this list linkage to redirect the testers' associations and feelings toward more suitable material. At the same time, this suggestive hint allows us to synthesise different people's feelings better and identify generic solutions through the test. We collected a total of 20 valid data from the online interviews and field interviews. We conducted a second round of sound tests on selected materials without revealing their shapes to the testers. Testers evaluated the sounds using a scale provided to them after feeling each material with their hands. This method reduced test errors and minimised the influence of other senses.
Here is the scale. In the second iteration, we collected 16 valid data and found that all materials scored above 3 on average, indicating a clear connection between them and the corresponding music. Although the degree of connection varies due to individual subjectivity, we believe the device offers a subjective experience that can provide a synesthesia-like experience for most people.
